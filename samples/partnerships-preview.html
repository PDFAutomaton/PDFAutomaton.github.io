⚡ Quick Win — Included Free
Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you touch a single acre, you need a Movement Baseline — a one-page snapshot of where deer are already trying to go on your property. Most landowners skip this step and spend years fighting deer behavior instead of working with it. This exercise takes 30 minutes, costs nothing, and is the single most important thing you can do before any habitat improvement. You're not building anything today. You're reading what's already there.

1
Pull up your property on Google Earth or onX and screenshot your land boundary

Open Google Earth Pro (free) or your onX Hunt app and zoom in on your property. Take a screenshot or print the satellite view. You need to see your terrain features clearly — timber blocks, open fields, creek drainages, thickets, and any neighboring land use like agriculture or hardwood edges adjacent to your line. If you've never looked at your property from above with fresh eyes specifically asking 'where would a deer want to travel,' you're about to see things you've walked past a hundred times.

2
Mark every pinch point, edge transition, and thick-to-open boundary you can identify

Using a pen on your printout or the markup tool in onX, circle or mark every place on your property where two different cover types meet — thick timber running into an open field, a creek bottom that narrows between two ridges, a brushy fencerow connecting two woodlots. These transition zones and pinch points are where deer movement naturally funnels. Don't overthink it. If it looks like a natural bottleneck or a bridge between cover, mark it. Aim for at least five marked locations on any property over 20 acres.

3
Rank your marked locations by how many deer signs you remember seeing there — even without fresh scouting

Next to each mark, write a number from 1 to 3 based on your gut memory: 3 means you've seen deer, tracks, rubs, or scrapes there multiple times across different seasons. 2 means you've seen sign once or twice but never hunted it seriously. 1 means it looks good on the map but you honestly don't have boots-on-ground confirmation yet. This ranking becomes your starting priority list. You're not guessing at random anymore — you're triaging your property based on evidence you already own.

✅ Expected result: In 30 minutes you'll have a rough but real Movement Baseline map — a marked-up aerial of your property showing where deer movement is likely funneling right now based on terrain and your own documented sign. This one page becomes the foundation every habitat decision in this guide gets built on. Landowners who do this step first stop wasting improvement dollars on areas deer were never going to use consistently. You'll refer back to this map throughout the entire planning process.

The Property Blueprint: Why 90% of Habitat Work Fails Before It Starts

Stop managing deer like you're hoping they show up and start managing land like you're engineering where they have to go.

Most Georgia landowners with 50-150 acres are making the same mistake: they're buying clover seed and hanging stands based on where they've seen deer before, which is reactive management that keeps you one season behind the deer's actual patterns. Before you plant a single food plot or cut a single shooting lane, you need to do one thing — pull up your property on onX Hunt or Google Earth and identify your three pinch points, your two bedding areas, and your one sanctuary (the place you never go, ever), because every habitat improvement you make for the next five years should be designed to funnel deer between those six locations. Your property size doesn't matter as much as your terrain transitions — a 40-acre property with a creek bottom, a hardwood ridge, and a field edge has more natural deer architecture than a 200-acre flat pine block, and understanding that distinction will save you thousands in wasted food plot inputs. The landowners who consistently kill mature bucks on small-to-mid acreage aren't outspending their neighbors, they're out-thinking them by treating their land as a system with entry points, travel corridors, and destination nodes — not just a collection of spots.

  • Open onX Hunt or Google Earth right now and drop pins on every location you've seen deer in the last two seasons — you're looking for a pattern of WHERE they were traveling, not just where they stopped
  • Identify the one area on your property that has the thickest nastiest cover and declare it your sanctuary by physically marking it on your map — no entry from August through January, no exceptions
  • Sketch a rough hand-drawn map of your property showing bedding to feed travel routes and identify the one natural bottleneck (creek crossing, fence gap, ridge saddle) that all those routes funnel through — that's your first habitat investment priority
💡 Pro tip: The biggest ROI move on any Southern property isn't a food plot — it's a hinge-cut thicket you create with a chainsaw in January that forces deer to bed exactly where you want them, which then makes every other habitat improvement you make downstream of that bedding area 3x more effective.

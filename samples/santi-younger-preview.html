Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you touch a single app setting or watch another tool tutorial, you need raw data on how you actually think. This exercise surfaces the real shape of your workflow in one sitting — and gives you a concrete starting point for everything else in this guide.

1
Run a 10-minute thought audit

Open a blank document — not Notion, not your usual app, just something with zero structure. Set a timer for 10 minutes and write out every type of thinking task you did yesterday: capturing an idea, planning something, processing information, communicating, creating. Don't organize it. Just list it fast and messy. You're looking for raw material, not a clean taxonomy.

2
Tag each item with how it actually felt to do it

Go back through your list and next to each item write one of three tags: F for Fluid (it felt easy and natural), R for Resistant (you did it but the tool or method created drag), or A for Avoided (you kept putting it off, and the friction was at least partly why). Be honest. An avoided task that you blamed on laziness might actually be a tool mismatch in disguise.

3
Identify your single biggest Resistant or Avoided pattern

Look at everything tagged R or A. Find the one that shows up most often or costs you the most time and mental energy. Write one sentence describing the gap: what the task requires versus what your current tool gives you. For example: 'I need to see how ideas connect spatially but my tool gives me a linear list.' That sentence is your first design brief — the specific problem your custom system needs to solve first.

✅ Expected result: You'll finish this exercise with a clear, honest picture of where your current setup is actually fighting you — not a vague sense that something is off, but a named, specific friction point you can start designing around today. Most people who do this realize within 20 minutes that they've been solving the wrong problem. That realization alone is worth the 30 minutes.

Why Your System Keeps Dying (And It's Not a Discipline Problem)

Discover why every productivity system you've tried has failed you — and build the diagnostic framework that finally makes your tools work with your brain instead of against it.

The reason your Notion setup became a graveyard and your to-do app stopped getting opened isn't laziness — it's a mismatch between how information actually moves through your brain and how the tool was designed to receive it. Most tools are built for a hypothetical 'organized person' who processes linearly, files immediately, and never has 47 browser tabs open about fermentation techniques, recipe testing notes, and half-finished article drafts simultaneously. Before you pick a single app or build a single template, you need to map your actual capture-to-output workflow: where do ideas hit you (voice memo in the kitchen, phone camera, random note at 2am), how long do they stay raw before you process them, and where does your work actually get finished. That honest audit — not another YouTube video about the perfect setup — is the only foundation that makes a system stick long-term.

  • Open your phone's notes app, last-used to-do app, and any abandoned Notion or Obsidian workspace and screenshot the graveyard — you need to see the pattern of where things went to die before you build anything new
  • For the next 24 hours, don't change any tool — just add a quick label to every task or idea you capture: write 'kitchen', 'desk', 'phone', or 'random' next to each one to map exactly where and when your brain produces work
  • Write three sentences (literally three, no more) answering: what's the ONE type of task I always complete without friction, what does that workflow look like, and what's different about it from everything else I abandon
💡 Pro tip: The system that sticks is almost never the most sophisticated one — it's the one with the shortest distance between 'idea hits you' and 'idea is captured', so if your current setup requires more than two taps or one click to log something while you're mid-recipe or mid-thought, the tool has already lost.

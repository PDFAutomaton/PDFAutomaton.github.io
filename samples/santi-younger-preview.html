The Custom Thinking System

Stop Fighting Your Tools, Build Ones That Fit

You have a note in Notion, a half-finished idea in your email drafts, a task in Todoist, a voice memo on your phone, and a tab open in your browser that you're 'definitely going to read later.' None of them are talking to each other. None of them reflect how the idea actually lives in your head. And somewhere between moving things from one app to another, the thinking itself stopped. That's not a discipline problem. That's a design problem.

This guide is about building a thinking system that fits the way your brain actually works—not the way productivity influencers say it should. That means something different for everyone. For some readers, it'll mean fewer tools and tighter integration. For others, it'll mean finally giving different kinds of thinking their own space without losing the thread between them. What it won't mean is adopting another someone else's framework and hoping this time it sticks. Every choice in here comes with the reasoning behind it, so you can take what applies and leave what doesn't. The goal is a system you stop maintaining and start using.

🎯 A thinking system that matches their actual brain, integrated enough that switching between writing/learning/task work feels seamless, flexible enough to evolve without abandonment

A note on where this comes from: I'm a researcher who spent several months studying how Santi's audience actually works—what they build, what they abandon, and why. I'm not Santi, and I'm not going to pretend this guide comes from a single person's genius system. It comes from patterns. Specifically, the pattern of intelligent people who are clearly capable of deep work but keep losing ground to their own tools. This guide is my attempt to document what the people who figured it out actually did differently. I've tried to keep my own opinions out of it and let the logic speak for itself.

⚡ Quick Win — Included Free
Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before anything else in this guide, do this one small thing. It won't complete your system. It'll do something more useful: it'll show you where your system is actually breaking. Most people who feel scattered aren't missing a better app. They're missing a clear picture of where their thinking currently lives and where it keeps getting lost. This exercise gives you that picture in under half an hour, using whatever you already have open on your desk right now.

1
Run a 10-minute capture audit

Open every tool you currently use—notes app, task manager, browser bookmarks, email drafts, voice memos, desktop folders, whatever. Don't organize anything. Just write down the name of each tool and one honest sentence about what kind of thinking ends up there. Not what it's supposed to be for. What actually ends up there. This gap between intention and reality is the first thing worth seeing clearly.

2
Find your one dead-end node

Look at your list and ask: which of these tools has things going in but nothing useful coming out? That's your dead-end node—the place where ideas go to stop being useful. It's almost always the biggest one, the most-featured app, the one you set up most carefully. Circle it. You're not deleting it. You're just naming the problem before trying to fix it.

3
Pick one idea that's been sitting there and move it somewhere it can become something

Take one note, thought, or half-idea from your dead-end node and move it somewhere it connects to actual work—a current project, an active document, a decision you need to make this week. Don't summarize it. Don't clean it up. Just relocate it somewhere it has context. Notice how that single move feels different from reorganizing. That feeling is the direction the rest of this guide goes.

✅ Expected result: You won't have a system yet. What you will have is a honest map of where your thinking currently breaks down, one less thing sitting in a dead-end, and a concrete sense of what integration actually feels like versus what organization feels like. That distinction is worth more than any template.

1

Map Your Actual Brain, Not the Brain Productivity Gurus Assume You Have

Before you build anything, you need a brutally honest map of how your thinking actually moves — not how you wish it moved.

Most systems fail food creators and knowledge workers not because the tools are bad, but because they're built around linear thinkers — and if you're the kind of person who connects a fermentation technique to a supply chain essay to a half-finished recipe video all in one sitting, you're not linear. Start by tracking your last five real work sessions and writing down every context switch you made, not to judge yourself, but to extract your actual thinking pattern — this is your cognitive fingerprint. The goal of Module 1 is not to install a system, it's to figure out what kind of system your brain is already running so you can build infrastructure around it instead of fighting it daily. Most people skip this step and spend years forcing themselves into someone else's workflow, wondering why it feels like wearing shoes on the wrong feet.

  • Open a blank doc right now and reconstruct yesterday's work session hour by hour — note every tool you touched, every topic you jumped to, and every moment you felt friction or flow
  • Identify your three most common thinking modes (example: 'research rabbit hole,' 'writing sprint,' 'task execution') and give each one a name you'd actually use
  • Write one sentence describing the single biggest moment each day where your current setup makes you lose momentum — this is the exact crack your new system will be built to fix
💡 Pro tip: The best thinking system for you probably already exists in fragments across your current mess — your job isn't to start from zero, it's to reverse-engineer why certain setups accidentally worked and make those accidents permanent.

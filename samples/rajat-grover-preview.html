Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you do anything else in this guide, do this one exercise right now. It takes 30 minutes and it will immediately show you whether your current trip budget is realistic — or whether you're about to hit a wall 60% of the way through booking. Most Indian travelers discover a ₹40,000 to ₹80,000 gap between their assumed budget and their actual cost during this exercise. Knowing that gap now costs you nothing. 1
Write down your current assumed total trip budget in rupees — right now, before calculating anything

Open your notes app or grab a piece of paper and write one number: what you currently think this trip will cost you, all-in, in INR. Don't calculate it yet, just write the number you have in your head. This is your baseline. Most people have a rough figure floating around — ₹1.2 lakh, ₹2 lakh, whatever yours is. Write it down and circle it. This number is going to be important in about 20 minutes.

2
Build a fast 6-line cost skeleton using Google Flights, Booking.com, and one honest food estimate

Open three tabs right now. Tab one: Google Flights — search your actual travel dates and note the real round-trip price in INR including baggage fees (add ₹4,000 to ₹8,000 if baggage isn't included). Tab two: Booking.com — filter for properties with a minimum 8.0 review score in a central neighborhood and note the per-night price for your dates. Multiply by number of nights. Tab three: Search 'average meal cost in [your destination] for tourists in 2024' and find a Reddit thread or travel forum post, not a travel blog. Multiply the daily food estimate by your trip days. Add these three numbers together. Then add 15% on top of that combined total to roughly account for local transport, sim card, entry fees, and incidentals. Write this new total next to your circled number from Step 1.

3
Compare your two numbers and identify your gap category

Look at both numbers side by side. If your new calculated number is within 10% of your original assumed budget, your instincts are well-calibrated and you're ready to do a proper full breakdown using the rest of this guide. If your calculated number is 20% to 40% higher than your assumption, you're in the most common gap category — and the rest of this guide will help you either close that gap through smarter booking or adjust your destination choice. If your calculated number is more than 40% higher, your destination or travel style expectations need a real conversation before you book anything, and Chapter 3 of this guide is specifically written for that situation. Either way, you now have real data in front of you instead of a number floating in your head.

✅ Expected result: Within 30 minutes you'll have a rough but honest cost skeleton for your trip in front of you, a clear sense of whether your current budget assumption is realistic, and most importantly, you'll have replaced the vague financial anxiety that comes from not knowing with an actual number you can work with. That shift — from 'I think this might cost around X' to 'I have evidence it will cost approximately Y' — is where confident trip planning actually begins.

The 'Asli Cost' Mindset: Why Every Indian Traveler Overspends Before Booking Anything

Shift from reactive price-checking to a proactive cost architecture mindset so you never again confuse 'cheap flight' with 'cheap trip'.

The single biggest reason Indian middle-class travelers from cities like Nagpur, Jaipur, or Coimbatore blow their international trip budget isn't the flight or the hotel — it's that they calculate cost in layers instead of all at once, booking the flight first and discovering the real total only at the airport. Think of your trip budget like a thali: the base plate is flights and hotels, but the dal, sabzi, roti, and dessert — local transport, visa fees, travel insurance, forex conversion losses, tipping culture, and food costs — can easily double that base number, and nobody tells you this upfront. The 'Indian tourist tax' is real but it's not always price gouging — half of it is self-inflicted because we compare our trip cost to a friend's 3-year-old Thailand trip without accounting for inflation, currency shifts, or that they stayed in a hostel dorm. Your new mental model is simple: never discuss or commit to any international trip until you've built what I call the Full Cost Stack — a single number that includes every rupee leaving your account from the day you start applying for a visa to the day you land back in India.

  • Open a new Google Sheet right now and create 8 columns labeled: Visa & Documentation, Flights, Accommodation, Local Transport, Food & Drinks, Activities & Entry Fees, Forex/Card Charges, and Emergency Buffer — this is your permanent Cost Stack template for every future trip
  • Go to your last international trip expense (or a friend's) and reverse-engineer what percentage each category was of the total spend — most Indians find food alone was 15-20% and they never budgeted for it separately
  • Write down the one international destination you're currently dreaming about, then Google the current Indian passport visa fee, approximate return flight cost, and daily hotel rate for 5 nights — add these three numbers and call this your 'Layer 1 number' which you'll build on in the next modules
💡 Pro tip: The most dangerous budget mistake Indian travelers make is anchoring their entire trip budget to the flight deal they found — a ₹25,000 return flight to Vietnam feels like a steal until you realize visa, food, transport, and activities in a tourist-heavy city like Hanoi will add another ₹40,000 minimum; always build your budget backwards from the destination's daily cost of living first, then find a flight that fits, not the other way around.

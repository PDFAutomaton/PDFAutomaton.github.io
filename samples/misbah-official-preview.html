The No-Nonsense Guide to Finding Your People, Keeping It Real, and Never Having to Choose Between Where You're From and Where You're At

⚡ Quick Win — Included Free
Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you read another word of this guide, do this one thing. It's called the 'Three Names Exercise' and it's specifically designed to break the mental fog that keeps diaspora guys stuck in surface-level friendships for years. You're not building a whole crew today — you're just identifying the seed. The person or people your crew grows from. Set a timer, make a coffee or grab some water, say Bismillah and set a clean niyyah that this is you investing in your akhira as much as your dunya — because the Prophet, peace be upon him, told us a man is on the religion of his close friend, so choosing well is literally an act of worship. Now go.

1
Write down every person you've spoken to in the last two weeks — just first names, no judgment

Grab your notes app or a piece of paper and list everyone: school, masjid, online, family friends, Somali community, teammates, anyone. Don't filter yet. You're just auditing your actual social world as it exists right now, not as you wish it was. Most guys are shocked to discover they've spoken to more people than they thought — the issue isn't access, it's depth. Give yourself eight minutes for this. If you get to prayer time, step away, pray, and come back — the reset will actually help you think clearer.

2
Circle the names where the conversation went past small talk at least once

Not 'we talked for a long time' — specifically, did it go somewhere real? Did someone mention something about family pressure, about feeling out of place, about a goal they have, about something that actually matters? Even one moment counts. These are your signals. You're looking for people who have shown even a small capacity for realness. In the Misbah world, these are the people who'd actually show up at the airport — not just react to the vlog of it. You might circle two names. You might circle five. Either is fine. Give yourself seven minutes.

3
Pick ONE name from your circled list and send them a specific, low-pressure message right now

Not 'hey' and not a voice note asking to hang out vaguely sometime. Something specific and real, like: 'Bro I was thinking — we should actually link up properly, not just see each other at [wherever]. You free this week for food or a walk or something?' That's it. No big reveal, no awkward confession of friendship. Just a direct, specific, low-stakes invitation that signals you're someone who makes things happen. The specificity is what separates guys who have tight crews from guys who always say they want one but never actually move. Send it before your thirty minutes is up. The barakah is in the action, not the intention alone.

✅ Expected result: Within 24 to 72 hours you will have either confirmed a real meetup with someone who has actual crew potential, or you'll have gotten a response that tells you clearly whether this person is worth investing more energy in. Either outcome is useful information. Most guys spend months in a fog about whether someone is actually a good friend candidate — this move forces clarity fast. You've just done more toward building a genuine crew in thirty minutes than most people do in a year of hoping it happens organically.

The Diaspora Identity Stack: Know Who You Are Before You Build Your Crew

Before you can find your ride-or-die people, you need to stop apologizing for being the guy who watches Misbah travel vlogs AND codes — your cultural duality is your superpower, not your weakness.

Most Somali guys in the West are running two operating systems at once — the version of you that fits in at school or work, and the real you that laughs at Somali pranks, feels something when you see airport reunion videos, and still calls your hooyo every few days — and the reason friendships feel shallow is because you're only ever showing people one of those. The Diaspora Friendship Code starts with one non-negotiable foundation: write down your three core identity anchors — these are the things that are true about you whether you're in Mogadishu, Melbourne, or Manchester — for most of this audience that means your deen, your Somali roots, and your specific passion (tech, content creation, gaming, whatever your lane is). Once you have those three anchors written down with a clear Bismillah and niyyah behind the exercise, you now have a filter — any friendship that requires you to dim or hide even one of those anchors is not a ride-or-die friendship, it's a performance, and performances are exhausting and temporary. The guys in Misbah's circle who show up in those vlogs, the airport moments, the surprise 100k celebrations — those friendships exist because there was a foundation of 'I see the real you and I'm still here,' and that starts with YOU being clear enough on yourself that others have something real to connect to.

  • Right now, open your notes app and write your three identity anchors — be brutally honest, not aspirational; if Somali culture and your deen are core, write that, don't write what sounds cool
  • Think of one person in your life — school, uni, online — who you've only ever shown your 'fitting in' face to, and identify one specific thing about your real self (a Somali value, a tech interest, something from your deen) you could authentically bring into your next conversation with them
  • Screenshot or save one piece of content — a vlog, a Somali meme, a tech video — that genuinely represents YOU at your most authentic, and use it as a visual reminder of your identity stack whenever you feel pressure to shrink yourself socially
💡 Pro tip: The biggest lie in diaspora social life is that you need to 'find your people' first and then be yourself — it's actually the reverse; the moment you operate from your identity anchors without apology, even online in comments or small group chats, you become a signal that other anchored people can locate, and they will find you faster than any forced social strategy ever could.

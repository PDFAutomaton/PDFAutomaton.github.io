The Graphics Analysis Framework

From AAA Breakdown to Your Game Engine: A Repeatable System for Reverse-Engineering Professional Techniques and Implementing Them in Your Own Projects

You watch a detailed breakdown of how Cyberpunk 2077 handles its wet street reflections — the screen-space reflections, the puddle normals, the way light bleeds across the asphalt at 2am — and for about twenty minutes, you genuinely feel like you understand it. Then you open Unreal Engine or Godot, stare at your scene, and realize you have no idea where to actually start. The knowledge evaporated somewhere between the video and the viewport. That gap — between understanding what a game is doing and knowing how to touch it in your own project — is exactly what this guide exists to close.

What you'll find here is not another theory dump about rendering pipelines or a glossary of terms you already half-know. This is a structured, repeatable framework for analyzing any AAA title systematically, identifying the specific techniques driving its visual quality, and translating those techniques into scaled, implementable decisions for your own engine and performance budget. We'll work through real examples — the kind of games already broken down on the World of Art channel, including titles like Horizon Forbidden West, Alan Wake 2, and The Callisto Protocol — using a consistent method you can apply to any game you sit down with. By the end, you will have a personal analysis toolkit: a set of checklists, priority filters, and translation steps that turn passive watching into active, buildable knowledge.

🎯 Be able to analyze professional games systematically, extract the core techniques used, and implement scaled versions in their own projects within realistic constraints

A quick note on where this guide comes from. I'm a third-party researcher and technical writer, not the creator behind World of Art. I've spent time studying the channel's visual breakdown methodology, cross-referencing it with game development documentation, GDC talks, and shader breakdowns from working technical artists, and what you're reading is my attempt to systematize what that channel does intuitively into something you can follow step by step. I have enormous respect for the work that goes into those video analyses, and this guide is built to complement that content — not replace it. I claim to have looked very carefully at how good technical analysis actually works, and written it down in a way that's actionable for the skill levels in this room.

Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you read another page, do this. Pick one game you already have installed — ideally something with a strong visual identity, anything from a modern open-world title to a recent horror game. You're going to run your first structured observation pass using just three focal points. This is the foundation of the full framework, and doing it once today in a low-stakes way will make every subsequent section click faster. You don't need to implement anything yet. You're training your eye to see with a method.

1
Boot the game and find one visually complex scene, then pause or enter photo mode

Look for a scene with mixed lighting conditions — ideally one with a strong light source, a shadowed area, and at least one reflective or wet surface. Interior doorways, street-level urban environments, and forest clearings at dusk all work well. Take a screenshot or just hold the frame. You want something you can stare at for five minutes without it changing. This is your specimen.

2
Write down exactly three observations using this sentence structure: I can see X, which is probably achieved by Y

Force yourself to name what you're seeing and make a guess at the technique behind it — even a wrong guess. For example: 'I can see the reflections on the floor breaking up near the edges of the screen, which is probably screen-space reflection with a falloff distance set.' Or: 'I can see the grass flattening in a circle around the player, which is probably a distance-based tessellation or wind simulation with a collision mask.' You are not graded on accuracy. You are building the habit of pairing observation with mechanism.

3
Google one of your three guesses using the exact technique name you wrote down, plus the words 'game dev implementation'

If you wrote 'screen-space reflection falloff,' search that. If you wrote 'grass collision mask Unreal,' search that. Spend ten minutes reading one result — a forum post, a GDC slide, a Reddit thread, anything. Then come back to your screenshot and see if the result changes how you read the image. It usually will. This loop — observe, name, research, re-observe — is the micro-version of the entire framework in this guide.

✅ Expected result: After 30 minutes you will have completed one full observation cycle on a real game scene, named at least three techniques by their approximate category, and connected one of them to an outside technical resource. You won't be able to implement anything yet, and that's exactly right. What you'll have is the beginning of a trained eye — one that looks at a game frame and immediately starts asking mechanical questions instead of just reacting to how good it looks. That shift in how you see is what everything else in this guide is built on.

The Reverse-Engineer's Eye: How to Actually See What AAA Games Are Doing

Stop watching graphics breakdowns passively and start extracting a reusable technical blueprint from every game you play.

Most people in this community can *describe* what makes Cyberpunk 2077 or Alan Wake 2 look incredible, but describing and dissecting are completely different skills — one keeps you a fan, the other makes you a developer. The Graphics Analysis Framework starts with a single habit: every time you notice a visual effect that stops you cold, you immediately ask three questions in sequence — What is the *input* data this effect needs, what is the *transformation* being applied, and what does the *output* look like in isolation from everything else around it. This input-transform-output loop is how you convert 'that subsurface scattering on skin looks insane' into an actionable technical spec you can chase in Unreal, Unity, or Godot, because you're no longer reacting emotionally to the whole image, you're surgically isolating one rendering pass at a time. The reason this framework works specifically for self-taught devs and junior engineers is that it bypasses the need for a graphics programming degree — you're leveraging the finished product as your textbook, and AAA studios have spent millions of dollars making that textbook as visually explicit as possible.

  • Today, boot up any game with high-fidelity lighting — Horizon Forbidden West, The Last of Us Part I, or even a game you already own — disable HDR on your monitor if possible, and spend 15 minutes doing nothing but pausing in scenes and naming exactly one rendering technique per screenshot you take (motion blur, ambient occlusion, bloom, etc.) — the act of naming forces your brain out of passive consumption mode
  • Go to the GDC Vault right now (gdcvault.com, free content is available without login) and find ONE rendering talk from a studio whose art style you want to replicate — don't watch the whole thing yet, just skim the slide deck and write down every technical term you don't fully understand, because that list IS your personal curriculum gap-analysis
  • Create a dead-simple 'Technique Teardown Doc' — a Google Doc or Notion page with three columns labeled Input, Transform, Output — and immediately populate it with one effect from your gaming session using your best guess for each column, even if you're wrong, because committing a hypothesis forces you to look for evidence that confirms or breaks it
💡 Pro tip: The biggest mistake this community makes is studying the most complex effects first because they're the most impressive — but your analysis muscle gets built on simple, isolated effects like a basic rim light or a depth-of-field blur, because when you can articulate those completely using the Input-Transform-Output model, you'll discover that 90% of 'impossible' AAA techniques are just three or four of those simple effects stacked with very specific parameter tuning, not alien technology.

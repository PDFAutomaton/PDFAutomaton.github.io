Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you go any further into this guide, do this one exercise. It's built around Schedule 4 of Guidance Note 3 — the visual inspection checklist that forms the mandatory pre-test phase of every EICR under BS 7671 Regulation 643.1. Most inspection failures and missed defects trace back to this phase being rushed or done in the wrong order. This exercise will show you exactly where your current visual check process is leaking time and missing items — and give you a repeatable sequence you can use on your next job.

1
Pull up or print the GN3 Schedule 4 visual inspection checklist and time your current process against it

If you don't have a copy of IET Guidance Note 3, use the NICEIC or NAPIT equivalent inspection schedule — any compliant EICR form will carry the same Schedule 4 items. Set a stopwatch. Work through a real consumer unit or distribution board in front of you — or if you're off-site, use your last completed EICR paperwork. Go item by item and mark honestly: did you physically check this, or did you assume it. Common misses include confirming presence and condition of fire barriers around cables at the board, verifying enclosure IP rating is appropriate for location under BS EN 60529, and checking that all conductors are correctly identified to current cable colour conventions per Regulation 514.3.

2
Identify your three most-skipped visual check items and write them on a card you keep with your test equipment

Everyone has blind spots. The point of this step isn't to feel bad about them — it's to make them impossible to skip again. The three most common skipped items reported by electricians preparing for EICR assessments are: condition and security of main earthing and bonding conductors (Regulation 411.3), presence of RCD quarterly test notice where required (Regulation 514.12.2), and evidence of overheating or arcing at terminations inside the board. Write your three on a card. Tape it inside your test kit bag. That card is your pre-test ritual from today forward.

3
Run your visual check sequence on one real or practice board using only the sequenced order in this guide's Chapter 2 — and note how long it takes versus your previous approach

The sequence matters because it mirrors the order in which defects affect test result validity. A missed bonding issue found after continuity testing means your R1+R2 results are potentially invalid and the sequence must be repeated. Visual checks are not a formality — they are the filter that makes your test data defensible. Time yourself using the Chapter 2 sequence. Most electricians who have done this exercise report cutting their visual phase time by 20 to 35 percent while actually catching more items, because the sequence eliminates backtracking and decision fatigue.

1

Why Smart Sparks Still Fail EICRs (And the Mental Reset That Fixes It)

Shift from reactive fault-chasing to systematic inspection thinking so you stop missing the obvious stuff that kills your reputation on-site.

Most electricians who struggle with EICRs aren't failing because they lack technical knowledge — they're failing because they treat inspections like fault-finding jobs, hunting for problems instead of methodically verifying compliance against BS 7671, and that mindset difference is what separates a C1 you miss from a C1 that gets you a callback. The EICR process has a specific logic built into it: visual inspection before any instrument touches the installation, because roughly 60-70% of codeables you'll ever issue are visible to the naked eye if you're actually looking with purpose rather than just ticking boxes to get to the Megger. Start treating every installation you inspect as if the previous electrician was trying to hide something — worn cable insulation behind trunking, undersized CPC connections buried in back-boxes, DIY modifications under floorboards — because statistically, on domestic rewires over 25 years old, they usually did cut corners somewhere. The examiners and experienced inspectors who mark City & Guilds 2391 assessments aren't looking for perfect paperwork; they're looking for evidence that you have a repeatable, defensible process you could explain under questioning, which means your methodology needs to be locked in before you ever walk onto a job.

  • Pull up the EIC/EIRC schedule from BS 7671 Appendix 6 right now and read Section 1 (extent and limitations) out loud — write in your own words what the inspector's actual legal obligation is versus what a repair obligation is, because most electricians blur this line and it costs them on assessments
  • On your next job today, do a 5-minute visual-only walk of one room before touching any test equipment and write down every potential codeable you spot with your eyes — then compare your list against what your instruments later confirm, and you'll immediately see where your visual discipline is weak
  • Write three sentences describing your personal inspection sequence — not the generic textbook order, but the actual route you take around a property and why — because if you can't articulate it, you don't have a system, you have a habit, and habits fail under pressure
💡 Pro tip: The single biggest differentiator between electricians who pass 2391 assessments first time and those who don't isn't technical knowledge — it's that successful candidates can justify every decision verbally in real time, so practice narrating your inspection out loud on every job for the next two weeks, even solo, because the examiner's favourite question is 'why did you do that in that order?' and silence kills marks faster than a wrong answer.

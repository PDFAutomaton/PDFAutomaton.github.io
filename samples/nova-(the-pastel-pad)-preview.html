Get Your First Result in 30 Minutes

Before you read another page, do this. Right now, today, within the next 30 minutes. This exercise is the foundation of the entire system, and completing it will immediately show you something about your studying that most students never figure out until after a failed exam. You only need a pen, one sheet of paper, and one subject you have coming up. Set your intention first — say Bismillah, take one breath, and remind yourself that seeking knowledge is an act of worship. That single moment of niyyah changes the quality of what follows.

1
Write down everything your next exam will test — from memory, without opening a single note.

Take your sheet of paper and spend exactly 10 minutes writing every topic, concept, formula, or skill you believe will appear on your next upcoming exam in any subject. Do not open your textbook, your notes, or your phone. Just write what you actually know is on there. This is not a test — there is no grade. What you are doing is creating a real map of what your brain has already stored versus what it only thinks it knows. Most students skip this step entirely and spend their study time re-reading things they already understand while completely ignoring the gaps that will cost them marks. When your 10 minutes are up, put your pen down.

2
Open your notes or syllabus and mark every gap — anything you missed, blanked on, or wrote vaguely.

Spend the next 10 minutes comparing your brain-dump list against your actual course notes, syllabus, or textbook chapter headings. Circle or mark anything you missed entirely. Put a small question mark next to anything you wrote down but only vaguely — the topics where you knew the name but couldn't explain the concept if someone asked you right now. Be honest. Nobody is watching and nobody is judging. What you are building is your personal priority list — the exact topics that, if you studied them first and most, would have the highest impact on your grade. This is already more strategic than most students ever get.

3
Write your top three priority topics on a sticky note and put it where you study.

From your gap list, choose the three topics that are most likely to appear on your exam AND that you understood the least clearly in step two. Write only those three on a sticky note or small piece of paper and place it on your desk, your laptop, or wherever you usually study. These are your anchors for every study session until that exam. You are not ignoring everything else — you are making sure your limited time and energy goes toward the areas with the highest return first. This is the core logic of the entire system you are about to learn, and you just applied it in under 30 minutes.

✅ Expected result: By the time you finish this exercise, you will have something most students never have before an exam: a clear, honest picture of where your actual knowledge gaps are right now, and a starting point that is specific enough to act on today. Students who complete this step before studying report feeling immediately less overwhelmed — not because there is less to cover, but because they finally know where to start. Keep that sticky note. You will use it in Chapter Two.

The Niyyah Shift: Why Your Study Mindset Is the Only Thing Standing Between You and Consistent 90s

Before you touch a single flashcard or open your notebook, you need to rewire the story you're telling yourself about studying — because scattered grades come from scattered thinking, not scattered time.

Most students in this community open their notes with zero intention set, which is why three tabs become fifteen and two hours disappear with nothing to show — your brain needs a declared mission before it can execute one, so starting every session with a genuine Bismillah and a written niyyah ('I'm studying this chemistry unit to understand rizq through knowledge and to honor my parents') literally activates a different mode of focus than 'I guess I should study.' The students who pull consistent 90s aren't smarter than you — they've simply built a belief that studying is something they *are*, not something they *do when exams panic them*, and that identity shift changes every micro-decision from whether to check Instagram to whether to rewrite that confusing paragraph one more time. You also need to stop romanticizing the aesthetic of studying (the pretty planner, the color-coded tabs Nova posts about) without installing the foundation underneath it — the system has to live in your values first, your notebook second, or it will collapse the moment Ramadan gets busy or sports season hits.

  • Right now, write ONE sentence on a sticky note that answers: why does my education matter to me beyond grades — connect it to your deen, your family, or your future community, and put it where you open your laptop
  • Before your very next study session, say Bismillah out loud and write a two-line intention that names the subject, the specific goal for that session, and who benefits from your knowledge — do this every single time until it's as automatic as wudu before salah
  • Audit your current 'study identity' by finishing this sentence honestly in your journal: 'I'm the kind of student who ______' — if your answer describes someone reactive and panic-driven, rewrite it as who you are *becoming* and read it before every session this week
💡 Pro tip: The most powerful focus reset you already have built into your day is salah — high performers in this community use the five prayer times as natural study block boundaries, so instead of fighting distraction and forcing 3-hour marathon sessions, structure your studying *between* prayers, making each block sacred and finite, which paradoxically produces more retention than the all-nighters that feel more productive.
